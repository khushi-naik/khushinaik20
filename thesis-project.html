Project Overview

I designed and developed an Augmented Reality (AR) display for patient vital alarms, aimed at improving situational awareness in clinical settings like ICUs—where traditional bedside monitors rely heavily on audio and peripheral screens. This system visualizes three critical vital signs—heart rate, blood pressure, and oxygen saturation (SpO₂)—using animated, color-coded 3D spheres that are always in the clinician’s field of view via HoloLens 2.

Problem & Inspiration
Alarm Fatigue & Noise Overload

Clinical environments like ICUs and ORs are filled with constant alarms and conversations, which can overwhelm clinicians and disturb resting patients.

Limited Visibility & Accessibility

Vital monitors are often not in the clinician’s direct line of sight, causing delays and distraction due to constant shifts in focus between patient and screen.

Challenges in Critical Field Settings

In military evacuations or space-constrained scenarios, bright screens and alarm sounds can compromise safety, while bulky equipment strains limited resources.

Ideation & Planning

This phase brought together real-world observations, user research, and early idea exploration to inform and shape our solution.

Observing Users

I feel lonely and homesick at times. My social interactions have been very limited...

Visited McGill's Student Wellness Hub to observe how students cope with stress.

Noticed students engaging in art activities like painting, sketching, coloring and Mandala as a form of emotional relief.

Building Personas

Art therapy holds value for individuals across ages and backgrounds, from students managing academic stress to seniors coping with loneliness. we created user personas that captured diverse emotional needs, daily routines, and motivations.

Brainstorming Solution

Inspired by collaborative art trends where multiple creators take turns contributing to a shared piece.

Initially proposed a turn-based digital art game where users join a shared session, draw one at a time with a time-limited turn, and only see the artwork when it’s their moment to contribute — fostering creativity, stress relief and spontaneity.

Later expanded the concept to include other art forms, local art therapy resources, and listings of nearby workshops to support broader mental wellness needs.

Iteration 1

10+10 Method

We used the 10 plus 10 method to explore various design ideas for our product. In the first 10 sketches (row 1-below), we majorly focused on exploring potential layouts of our homepage and in the next 10 sketches (row 2), we fine-tuned a couple of these homepage design ideas and also looked into some of the possible user interactions with respect to the application features.

Application Workflow

We sketched out the core application flow to outline how users would interact with the following key features:

  • Art-Based Games – A collection of creative games designed for relaxation. This includes our custom-designed turn-based collaborative drawing game.

  • Therapist Resources – A directory that helps users discover and connect with certified art therapists.

  • Workshop Listings – Upcoming virtual or local art therapy sessions and events to encourage participation in creative communities.

  • User Accounts – A simple account creation process.

Low-Fidelity Paper Prototypes

We then sketched out a couple of low-fidelity paper prototypes to visualize the key flows of our application. These prototypes helped us quickly test layout ideas, interaction flows, and feature placements. They were instrumental in gathering initial user feedback and identifying usability issues before moving into high-fidelity prototype designing.

What I Learned

Designing haptic feedback requires extra care — its effects aren’t always obvious, so clarity and intention are critical.

Gathering user feedback is invaluable — it often reveals usability issues you may not anticipate during development.

Sketching interface ideas early in the process helps explore a range of possibilities before committing to one direction.
