HapTutor: A Haptic Tutor for Improving Freehand Drawing Skills

COURSE

Human-Computer Interaction

TIMELINE

~ 4 months

MY ROLE

Designing the prototype and conducting user-study

Overview

The goal was to explore how haptic feedback can help novice artists improve freehand drawing — a skill that takes years of practice to master. Using the Haply 2Diy device, I designed and tested a haptic training application focused on basic shapes like lines and circles.

Problem & Inspiration

What did we tackle?

For this project, I had the freedom to explore any topic of interest and I naturally steered toward the art space.

Freehand drawing, while seemingly simple, takes a lot of practice to master.

I wanted to create something that could help learners improve their control and confidence — especially in a way that supports independent, self-paced practice.

While experimenting with Haply’s 2Diy haptic device, the idea clicked: what if I could combine tactile feedback with guided drawing exercises

Designing the Solution

Built on the idea that complex drawings comprise of basic shapes.

Through user input and observation, circles and lines emerged as the most challenging.

Sketched a few interface ideas, aiming to keep the layout clean and minimal.

Reviewed relevant literature to understand different types of haptic feedback.

Application Interface

What did it end up looking like?

Interface

Built with Processing (Java), the application allowed users to -

  • Select from a menu of basic shapes (lines, circles) to draw
  • Use the provided drawing cues like start/end points while training

Haptic Feedback Modes

Two types of force feedback were implemented using Haply’s 2Diy -

  • Corrective Feedback: Nudges the hand toward the ideal stroke
  • Disruptive Feedback: Applies random resistance to keep users alert

User Study

    Five participants underwent 2 rounds of haptic training followed by a test after each round.

    Results showed improved accuracy in line drawing; circle results were less consistent.

    Post study interviews pointed to areas for refinement.

    Limitations included weak visual cues for circles, and occasional jerky haptic feedback

My Learnings

Designing haptic feedback requires extra care — its effects aren’t always obvious, so clarity and intention are critical.

Gathering user feedback is invaluable — it often reveals usability issues you may not anticipate during development.

Sketching interface ideas early in the process helps explore a range of possibilities before committing to one direction.
